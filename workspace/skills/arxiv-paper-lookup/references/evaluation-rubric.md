# Evaluation Rubric

Use this rubric when the user asks for ranking, scoring, critique, or a paper review.

Score each dimension on a 1–10 scale and justify each score with short evidence-based notes.

## Dimensions

### 1. Problem Significance
Assess whether the paper addresses an important, difficult, or high-impact problem.

Consider:
- importance of the problem to the field
- breadth of potential relevance
- whether the paper solves a real bottleneck or only a narrow edge case
- whether the motivation is compelling and well supported

Guide:
- 9–10: major and broadly important problem
- 7–8: clearly meaningful problem with solid importance
- 5–6: moderate or niche significance
- 3–4: limited significance or weak motivation
- 1–2: trivial or poorly justified problem

### 2. Methodological Innovativeness
Assess how novel, creative, and technically interesting the method is.

Consider:
- whether the method is clearly distinct from prior work
- whether it combines known ideas in a genuinely useful way
- whether the technical design demonstrates originality
- whether the claimed novelty appears substantial or incremental

Guide:
- 9–10: highly original and technically inventive
- 7–8: clearly novel with meaningful innovation
- 5–6: moderately novel or a solid recombination of known methods
- 3–4: mostly incremental
- 1–2: little visible novelty

### 3. Conclusion Value
Assess how valuable, credible, and actionable the paper's conclusions are.

Consider:
- strength of empirical or theoretical support
- whether conclusions match the evidence
- practical usefulness of the findings
- whether limitations materially weaken the contribution

Guide:
- 9–10: strong, credible, and highly useful conclusions
- 7–8: valuable conclusions with good support
- 5–6: mixed or moderately useful conclusions
- 3–4: weakly supported or limited conclusions
- 1–2: unconvincing conclusions

### 4. Author Reputation
Assess author reputation cautiously and only from available evidence.

Consider:
- explicit evidence in the paper or fetched public sources
- whether the authors appear established in the topic area
- whether affiliations or prior work are directly evidenced

Rules:
- do not invent citation counts, awards, or prestige
- if evidence is weak, say so explicitly
- low evidence should reduce confidence, not force a low score by itself

Guide:
- 9–10: strong directly evidenced reputation in the area
- 7–8: good evidence of relevant credibility
- 5–6: some signs of credibility but limited evidence
- 3–4: weak evidence of topic-specific reputation
- 1–2: no reliable evidence or concerning signals

## Overall Ranking

After scoring all dimensions:
- provide an overall 1–10 ranking
- explain the main drivers of the overall score
- include a confidence label: high, medium, or low

Suggested overall verdict labels:
- 9.0–10.0: must-read
- 8.0–8.9: strong
- 7.0–7.9: promising
- 5.5–6.9: mixed
- below 5.5: weak

## Scoring Discipline

- Base judgments primarily on paper content.
- Distinguish summary from evaluation.
- Be conservative when evidence is incomplete.
- If author reputation cannot be reliably assessed, mark it provisional.
- Do not let one flashy claim dominate the full ranking.
